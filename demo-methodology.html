How Cultural Defaults Work

When you select a target language in the Conteranto demo, the four cultural dimension sliders automatically adjust to reflect that culture's typical communication norms. These defaults are computed from established cross-cultural research, not hand-picked.

Research Frameworks

Hofstede's Cultural Dimensions

Geert Hofstede's cultural dimensions theory, developed from a study of IBM employees across 76 countries (1967-1973) and extended in Cultures and Organizations: Software of the Mind (3rd ed, 2010), provides country-level scores on several cultural axes. We use three:

  • Power Distance (PDI) -- the degree to which less powerful members of a society accept that power is distributed unequally. High PDI cultures expect deference to authority.
  • Individualism (IDV) -- the degree to which individuals are expected to look after only themselves and their immediate family. Low IDV (collectivist) cultures emphasize group harmony and face-saving.
  • Uncertainty Avoidance (UAI) -- the degree to which members of a society feel uncomfortable with ambiguity. High UAI cultures prefer formal rules and structured communication.

Hall's Context Theory

Edward T. Hall's framework from Beyond Culture (1976) classifies cultures along a spectrum from low-context (meaning is stated explicitly) to high-context (meaning is embedded in context, relationships, and nonverbal cues).

Note: Hall did not assign numerical scores to countries. Our context (CTX) values are estimates based on Hall's qualitative framework, Erin Meyer's relative positioning in The Culture Map (2014), and regional communication research. These estimates are transparent and shown in the data table below.

The Formula

Each of Conteranto's four cultural dimensions is computed as a weighted combination of Hofstede's scores and our context estimate:

Politeness = 0.35 × PDI + 0.30 × (100 - IDV) + 0.35 × CTX
Directness = 0.40 × IDV + 0.35 × (100 - CTX) + 0.25 × (100 - PDI)
Formality = 0.35 × PDI + 0.35 × UAI + 0.30 × CTX
Attribution = 0.55 × IDV + 0.25 × (100 - PDI) + 0.20 × (100 - CTX)

Why these mappings?

Politeness -- Hierarchical societies (high PDI) use more deference markers. Collectivist cultures (low IDV) prioritize face-saving. High-context communication relies on implicit courtesy.

Directness -- Individualist cultures (high IDV) value explicit self-expression. Low-context communication requires stating things plainly. Egalitarian societies (low PDI) allow direct challenges.

Formality -- Hierarchical societies (high PDI) expect formal registers. High uncertainty avoidance (high UAI) prefers structured, rule-following communication. High-context cultures use elaborate forms.

Attribution -- Individualist cultures (high IDV) use "I" statements and active voice. Egalitarian societies (low PDI) express personal responsibility. Low-context communication names actors explicitly.

Linguistic Adjustments

Some languages grammaticalize cultural dimensions beyond what country-level scores capture. For example, Japanese has keigo (a multi-level politeness system built into verb conjugation), Persian has ta'arof (an elaborate courtesy system), and Korean has six distinct speech levels tied to social hierarchy. These linguistic structures amplify certain dimensions independently of Hofstede's country scores.

We apply small corrections (+3 to +10 points) for languages with such features. These adjustments are listed in the data table below.
